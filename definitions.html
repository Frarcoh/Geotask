Population River Coast

United Nations: an organisation created in 1945 and made up of delegates from almost all countries of the world. It deals with issues of global importance including those related to global peace and development.

Over-populated: when a country or region does not have enough resources to keep its people at a reasonable standard of living.

Under-populated: when a country or region has more resources available than are being used by the people living there.

Death rate: the number of deaths per year per 1000 population.

Birth rate: the number of live births per year per 1000 population.

Net migration: the difference between the number of people moving into a country (immigrants) and the number of people moving out of a country (emigrants).

LEDCs: less economically developed countries as defined by the Brandt report (1980).

MEDCs: more economically developed countries as defined by the Brandt report (1980).

Land-locked: an area or country whose borders are surrounded by land so there is no direct access to a sea or ocean.

Sedentary: farmers who are settled in one particular place.

Nomadic: people who move from place to place seasonally to find good land.

Fertility rate: in a country or region the average number of children a woman will have in her lifetime.

HIV/AIDS: human immunodeficiency virus is a sexually transmitted infection. The virus attacks the immune system when this stops working the person has acquired immune deficiency syndrome. This is usually fatal.

Colony: a country that has been taken over and ruled by another country.

Quality of life: the happiness, well-being and satisfaction of a person. Among the many factors that influence quality of life are the person's family, income and access to services.

Standard of living: the factors that affect a person's quality of life and which can be measured. Many measures to do with a person's standard of living are to do with material possessions.

Emigrants: people who permanently leave one country to live in another.

Demographers: a person who studies human populations - their size, distribution and composition.

Census: an official count of the population carried out by a government at regular intervals.

Dependency ratios: a relationship between the number of people of working age (15-64) and the young dependents (0-14) and old dependents (65+) who depend on them for e.g. benefits, pensions.

Population distribution: the way in which the population is spread out over an area, region, country, continent or the world.

Population density: the average number of people in a given area e.g. per km2.

Peninsula: a piece of land jutting into the sea and almost surrounded by it.

Access: how easy it is to obtain goods or services. It can be measured in terms of cost, time or distance.

GDP (gross domestic product) per person: the total value of all the goods and servies produced in a country in one year by all the people liivng in that country.

Drought: a long, continuous period of dry weather where any rainfall is below the average expected.

Subsistence agriculture: a means of supporting life of being able to meet one's own basic needs of food, water and shelter.

Precipitation: any form of water falling on the Earth's surface from the atmosphere. The main categories are dew, fog, frost, hail, mist, rain, sleet and snow.

Discharge: the amount of water flowing across the width of a river at a given point.

Drainage basin: the area of land drained by a river and its tributaries. This is also known as the catchment area.

Watershed: the boundary of a drainage basin. It is also knows as a divide.

Vertical erosion: where a stream or river flowing quickly down a steep gradient in an upland area wears away the bed at a faster rate than the valley sides, leading to a narrow V-shaped valley.

Lateral erosion: where a stream or river wears away the sides and banks on each side of the channel.

Capacity: the total amount of sediment or load that a stream or river can carry at any one time.

Competence: the maximum size of particle that a stream or river can carry.

Gorge: a deep, narrow valley with steep, rocky sides often with a stream or river flowing through it.

Delta: a landform, often triangular in shape, which develops where a river meets a slow body of moving water such as a lake or ocean. Sediment builds up above the water level forcing the river to split into distributaries to form a delta.

Distributaries: a stream or river channel resulting from the division of a larger stream channel. They are usually found in a delta.

Hydrographs: a graph which shows the pattern of a river's discharge. It is measured in cubic metres per second over a period of time.

Bay: a low-lying of land on the coast.

Headland: a high area of land that extends out into the sea.

Wave-cut platform: a flat area in front of a cliff, just below the low tide mark. These were formed when the waves eroded the cliff, but left a flat platform behind.

Wave-cut notch: an area of erosion cut the base of a cliff formed by the waves.

Longshore drift: the zigzag movement of sediment along a shore caused by waves going up the beach at an oblique angle (swash) and returning at right angles (backwash).

Erosion: the wearing away of pieces of rock, soil or other solid materials.

Deposition: the laying down of solid material such as mud or sand on the sea floor and river or lake beds.

Constructive waves: these waves are low and have a long wavelenght. They are low energy waves that have a strong swash and weak backwash. They are usually associated with beach deposition.

Destructive waves: these waves are high and steep with a short wavelenght: They are high energy waves that have a weak swash and strong backwash. They are usually associated with beach erosion.

Sedimentary rock: material that has been deposited by the force which originally carried it from elsewhere e.g. wind, water or ice. It can also consist of the accumulated remains of dead sea animals e.g. limestone.

Swash: the advance of a sea wave up a beach after the breaking of a wave.

Backwash: the retreat of a sea wave down the beach after the breaking of a wave.
